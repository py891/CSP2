One-line Premise

Cities heat up because many built materials have low albedo, high thermal mass, and low evapotranspiration, increasing surface temperatures and indoor cooling demand.

  • Physics: energy balance + radiation + heat capacity
  • Chemistry: molecular absorption + coatings + phase change
  • Biology: evapotranspiration + green infrastructure
  • ESS: systems view + feedback loops + policy
  • CS: structuring content + data presentation + website

Project Overview

What we investigated and why it matters.

Built environments replace natural land covers (soil, vegetation, water) with engineered materials such as asphalt, concrete, and roofing membranes. These surfaces differ in reflectivity (albedo), heat storage (thermal mass), conductivity, and ability to support water-driven cooling. As a result, urban surfaces can reach extreme temperatures, contributing to the Urban Heat Island effect and increasing demand for indoor cooling â€” raising electricity use, cost, and emissions.

Driving Question

How do surface materials influence temperature and energy demand in built environments?

Outcome

Compare how material properties change heating/cooling behavior and evaluate mitigation strategies (cool roofs, reflective pavements, green roofs).
